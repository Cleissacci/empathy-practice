Model Response Key

Exemplar answers for the four AI roleplay simulations. Each one demonstrates the target skill and satisfies every item on its empathy rubric.

Facilitator copy — not for learners

How to use this

The simulations are live, unscripted conversations — the AI character responds to whatever the learner actually says, so there is no single correct script. These models show one strong way to meet each rubric, useful for calibrating scores, debriefing learners, or demonstrating the skill.

Character names differ by mode: the workplace Story mode uses Maya, Devon, Priya and Theo; standard mode uses the same first three names and Riley for the capstone. The responses below apply to either.

01

The last-minute cancel

Perspective Taking · The generous interpretation

Maya texts to cancel your plans an hour before.

“Hey… I can’t make it tonight. I’m so sorry to do this last minute.”

Model learner response No worries at all, Maya — honestly, don’t stress about it. You’d been looking forward to this too, so I figure something must’ve come up. Everything okay on your end? I’m around if you want to talk, and we’ll find another night.
Doesn’t assume a slight Leads with warmth Curious about the unseen Keeps the door open

Avoid: “Wow, again?” / scorekeeping / silently deciding they don’t value you.

02

Feeling overlooked

Active Listening · Reflecting

Devon, voice tight, says his work goes unseen.

“I just feel like… no one even reads my reports.”

Model learner response That sounds really discouraging — like you’re putting real care into them and it’s just disappearing into a void. It’s been getting to you, hasn’t it? Tell me more about what that’s been like.
Names the feeling No quick reassurance Holds off on fixing Shows he was received

Avoid: “I’m sure people read them” (reassurance) / “Try making them shorter” (jumping to fix).

03

The heat of the moment

Difficult Conversations · Regulate, then relate

Priya challenges your decision sharply, in front of the team.

“I don’t agree with this call, and I don’t think you ran it past any of us first.”

Model learner response Okay — let me take a breath, because I can feel myself getting defensive and I don’t want to react from there. You’re right that I moved on this without looping you in, and I can see why that landed badly. Help me understand what you’re most worried about — I genuinely want to hear it.
Regulates self first Doesn’t escalate Turns toward Acknowledges her view

Avoid: pressing your point harder / matching her volume / defending before you’ve steadied yourself.

04

Passed over

Capstone · All three skills, one conversation

Riley (or Theo) was passed over for a role you helped decide. The conversation moves through three phases — a strong run meets the rubric in each.

Phase 1 · They arrive guarded

Model learner response I’m really glad you came to me. I can only imagine how this landed, especially after everything you’ve poured in. I’m not here to defend anything — I just want to hear you.

Phase 2 · They open up

Model learner response It sounds like this isn’t only about the role — it’s the worry that the work, and you, might be invisible here. That’s a heavy thing to be carrying.

Phase 3 · The challenge — “Did you even advocate for me?”

Model learner response That’s a fair question, and you deserve a straight answer. I did advocate for you — and I also should have spoken with you before the announcement, not after. That part’s on me. I don’t want to lose you, and I want to be honest about where things actually stand.
Held a generous interpretation Stayed curious Named the feeling underneath Didn’t rush to fix Stayed regulated under pressure Turned toward & acknowledged

Avoid: justifying the decision early / pep-talking the hurt away / pulling rank or getting defensive when challenged. Passing the capstone needs 70%+.
